Wine Score Analysis

To further guide you in your search for your preferred wine, we thought it would be useful examine what attributes contribute to a highly rated wine.
The following explains the output from a regression analysis using different variables in the dataset as predictors and the wine score as the response.
We start off with the most obvious predictor, price. Is expensive wine worth the dollar?
We see above a scatterplot with the x-axis representing price, and the y-axis representing points, or wine score. The line that runs across diagonally is our regression line, which seems to indicate that generally, pricier wine tends to receive higher scores.
Next, we examine by country. A positive value(bar pointing to the right) means that on average, wine produced in that country has been awarded extra points compared to the average wine.
We can see that Austria and Germany have been awarded the highest scores according to this model.
By province, we see the largest average gains in scores from the Burgundy and Piedmont provinces.
The Mendoza Province, Northern Spain, and Veneto have not fared well according to this model.
In terms of variety, Pinot Noir and Riesling have been awarded higher scores on average.
Rose, Merlot, and Sauvignon Blanc have not.
Finally, by winery, the Williams Selyem winery has received the highest scores on average, followed by Testarossa and Louis Latour.
